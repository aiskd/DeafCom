Purpose

This website aims to provide information about sign language to hearing parents with deaf children and also support them through their learning experience. Moreover, this website will act as a bridge for families with hearing parents and deaf children to start learning sign language, only if they wish to do so.

So.. What is sign language?

Sign language is a visual language used to communicate with one another through gestures. Similar to spoken languages, sign language has its own grammar and structure and also differ from country to country. While America uses ASL (American Sign Language), Australia uses AUSLAN (Australian Sign Language) as their native sign language.

How is sign language beneficial to you and your family?

You must be wondering what exactly can sign language do for you and your family or is it worth going through the trouble to learn it? Here are some advantages sign language can give you:

Improves Language Development
Increases Parent-Child Communication
Become More Involved with the Deaf Community

Improves Language Development

Since sign language is a visual language and does not require listening to sounds, it is fully accessible for deaf children. By learning sign language, children will be able to fill in the communication gaps, which is when children are unable to understand a certain area of language, or it can also assist children to thoroughly understand how communication works. Hence, children will have the ability to have a normal or an enhanced language development process compared to their hearing peers. (Caselli et al., 2021)

Learning sign language also provides children a stable base of language comprehension, as deaf children find it easier to learn sign language compared to spoken languages. Due to this steady foundation of language, it allows deaf children to have a better grasp on how spoken languages work.

Increases Parent-Child Communication

As sign language requires eye contact to communicate with each other, children may feel as though they are being given proper attention. Therefore, they become more encouraged to communicate more with the person they are currently socializing with.

When deaf children first start learning spoken language, they may feel excluded from conversations that are done in spoken language. This is due to the fact that they can feel a language barrier between them and the speaker.

Imagine this: your family decided to move to a foreign country as your parents wished to share their culture to you. As a result, they decided to constantly speak their foreign language to you, who has no idea how to speak or understand this language. You slowly start to make a bit sense of what your parents are talking about, but their conversations still sound like gibberish and sounds to you.

What would you feel if you actually experienced something similar to this? Most would say they feel as if they are being excluded and unwanted from the conversation, which led to them becoming less engaged in communicating.

Become More Involved with the Deaf Community

By learning sign language, a bridge of communication with the Deaf community can be formed. This lets deaf children to socialize with other people who also experience deafness. Hence, children are able to learn more and bond with people who have experienced Deaf culture. This also allows children to embrace their identity as a deaf person.

Being more involved with the Deaf community allows children to share their struggles and get advice from those in the Deaf community, as deaf children feel as if their parents may not entirely understand how they feel as they are not deaf themselves. This does not mean children care less about their parents, as they are not deaf, but it shows that they feel more comfortable conversing with someone who has had the same experiences as them. It also brings them comfort knowing that there are also people like them.

"Sign language prevents deaf children from learning the spoken language properly"

It has been often argued that sign language can risk children's language development. However, it is reported that there is no evidence for this claim. In fact, evidence for the opposite can be found instead. Due to sign language being a visual language, it is fully accessible for deaf children. In contrast, when children learn spoken languages, they may still experience gaps in their development as the information from the spoken language is not reaching and difficult to process for the child.

"Deaf children learn language the same way hearing children do."

Deaf children are able to develop language as well as hearing children when they are exposed to fluent sign language from birth. Sign language allows children to comprehend the idea of language and will then have a stable base of understanding when learning a new language.

This problem can be compared to a situation where children are being taught addition of numbers without understanding the concept of numbers. Some children may be able to understand it right away. However, some children may experience trouble in learning it as they do not have a basic comprehension of the concept.

"Learning sign language would isolate my child from society"

It is true that deaf people are often prejudiced from society and as a result, become isolated due to their differences and language barrier. However, sign language can help children become more included into the Deaf community instead. This is due to the fact that sign language can only be understood by those in the Deaf community. Therefore, by learning sign language it shows that you are willing to put effort into integrating in the Deaf community.
